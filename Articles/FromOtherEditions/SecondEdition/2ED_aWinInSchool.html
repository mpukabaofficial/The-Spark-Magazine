A Win In School: Your Best Bet | Part 1

By Mphumelelo Khaba

I don’t know about everyone, but education is constantly getting frustrating and unpleasant with time. I do not know if it is because I have been doing this for over a decade but there is something about it. “Why should we spend the peak of our lives in a routine that teaches us things that we will not need in the future?”
“When am I going to use integration when I buy milk in the store?”
“How do tectonic plates feed me when I am hungry?”
“For goodness sake why do I need to wake up early to just learn physics?”
If this for getting a good job, why do we have to learn so much? People aren’t happy with their jobs and the job market is looking grim, what if all this is for nothing? School is frustrating for most people, it is boring for many and ultimately useless for the unemployed in the country. So why bother? It took a decade for amazon to be the billion dollar company it is now, some people hardly scratch around E200 000 (14 000 US$) annually yet they have been to school for over two decades. It is discouraging, to some, very unfair.

First of all, I think has been corrupted by the association of education and our survival. To oppose myself early: rightfully so, if survival without education was easy and/or by default possible, then people would not be incentivised to learn, slowly brewing a people of total ignorance. However, the there is joy in education that, unfortunately, we might not be able to appreciate in our lifetime. One of the issues of education it is wrapped around expensive and ridiculous tuitions fees, books and for some, uniforms. It also creates a certain expectations by the payer to the scholar. It results in tensions and conflict when the student is not doing so well. Consequently, this makes education a high stakes experience for academics.

Education is a key factor in reducing global poverty, according to the United Nations. Although wealth disparity exists, poverty has significantly decreased over the past 50 years. The argument that some billionaires because they did not complete formal education they are successful, is very misleading. First, that is not the point! The point of education is not success but it is like training a soldier for war and as graphic as that sounds, the idea is that people can become better citizens and potentially contribute into the longevity of our species and its pursuit of the truth. Also, learning is something our brain enjoys doing. It is no wonder we have so many theories about how things work as we try to navigate the world. Education is an instrument for understanding our world and it happens to be also a path to our respective success, whatever success means for each one of us.

Acting naïve isn’t a solution because education is a path to getting a job a making a living. If one does not invest their time in school and aim to get good grades they are unlikely to foster a successful career. Some people are able to make it through, because of their innovation, luck, and hard work without having to complete the full course of the journey of school, but that is a significant minority of people. Contrary to that, if you think you are special and will get out of poverty without school – go right ahead – but if you are being realistic and have not shown capabilities that have been rewarded by money and status, probably you ought to go to school because it increases your chances considerably. Knowing our country, eSwatini, this could never have been truer.

In conclusion, the value of education, while debated due to its flaws and financial burdens, is undeniably fundamental for personal growth and societal progress. While some succeed without formal education, these are exceptions rather than the rule. For most, education broadens opportunities and enhances life prospects. In eSwatini, education is vital for both individual success and national development, equipping citizens to tackle local and global challenges. It's not just a win for students, schools and the country, but a triumph for humanity.
