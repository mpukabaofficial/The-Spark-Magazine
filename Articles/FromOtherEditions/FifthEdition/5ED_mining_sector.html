The government of Eswatini has to cut off its monopolistic hand over the mining sector if it wants the economy to sky-rocket!

By Mlandvo-Sikhumbuzo kaMavimbela

Last edited: December 09, 2023

Against the backdrop of 70% of the population living below the poverty line and unemployment so rife, it is quite disappointing how the government still insists on having a huge medley of monopoly in the minerals and mining industry. The country has huge potential of being a lucrative mining powerhouse if only the government could consider detaching its huge domination over the minerals market. Foreign Direct Investment could go on an upward trajectory if only the government eased the 50% control of the mining terrain of the country.

LEGISLATION ALLOWS GOVERNMENT MONOPOLY IN THE SECTOR

Home to the oldest known and operational mine in the world, the lovely country of ours is astonishingly lagging behind in terms of growth and development of the minerals resources sector. The main reason for the sluggish mining sector is the deliberate monopoly that the government has over the sector through legal instrumentations including the Mines and Minerals Act of 2011. This piece of legislation gives the state and its head the lucratively rewarding courtesy of having 50% direct control of every mine in the county. Section 133 of the statute stipulates that “ (1) The Ingwenyama in trust for the Eswatini Nation shall acquire 25 per cent shareholding without any monetary consideration in a large-scale mining project for which a mining licence is granted.” The other 25% that the act makes provisions to is for the state, leaving the company that is granted a mining license in the country only left with 50% shareholding. This statute makes the illusion that the King is holding the 25% shareholding in benefit to the ordinary LiSwati, which in all its shades is untrue and an unlived reality! What is an unquestionable fact is that the ordinary Swati citizen does not have leeway to any benefits of profits in the mining sector as section 133(1) of the main mining legislative piece claims. 70% of the population continues to languish in dire poverty while the government smiles all the way to the bank in gross profits.

THE HISTORY OF MINING COMPANIES FLEEING

Salgaocar Mine PTY (LTD) was a beaming lighthouse of hope and employment for many Ngwenya Residents. The iron ore mining institution was unarguably the biggest mine in the country which made the biggest revenue returns in the local mining market. Themba Msibi, who was one of the heavy equipment operator in the mine now owned by Mwelase Mining Eswatini recalls how the closure and seizing of operations by SALGAOCAR in 2014 affected him and his family. “I had 2 children whom I supported in tertiary when the sudden news that the mine was going to close broke”. The infamous closure of the mine rendered Msibi along with a multitude of his former colleagues unemployed and without any manners of making ends meets. The government’s 50% rule in the mining industry is one of the reasons that can be attributed as the leading factors that steered the closure of the mine. Unemployment is disastrous for an economy of any country. If the sector operated in a free-market, job creation would in surplus, investors would come flocking in and the GDP would shoot in an appealing up. The government’s “bully-big brother” in the sector is one of the reasons why the sector will not grow and revive the economy. So in other words, the government is delaying grand employment and mass fiscal development by furthering its monopolistic dogma in the industry.

The number of mining companies making an exit due to the 50% monopoly-driven prerequisite is pure testament to the level of dissatisfaction Foreign Investors have with the government’s policy when it comes to minerals control and mine ownership. Just the other day, Prince Lonkhokhela, the new Minister of Mineral Resources made a grand announcement of a recent discovery of gold deposits in the country. Who will own the means of production? The state! The very state that claims to have our best interests at heart will own the means of production of the new gold mine, me and you will continue to be audiences on the sidelines while the government continues to make tonnes of profits while claiming to hold the minerals in our trust as the Minerals Act claim.

The Country can undergo an exodus of industrial development and economic resuscitation if only the government can reevaluate its policy stance on the minerals industry. The sector has huge potential of being the leading contributor to the economy. For how long are we going to depend on sugar solely?? It’s the 21st century and our economy is still based on the resilience of the sugar industry. Its time for an industrial change. Mining has the ability of changing our economic misfortunes as a country. We cannot continue being a country whose economy rests on the tiny granules of sugar! Working closely with the new Ministry of Justice, the government has to setup a parliamentary portfolio committee that will look into ways of transforming the minerals sector in the country. One way is to do away with legislative statutes and clauses that permits this lethal governmental monopoly. Develop legal frameworks that will enable a free minerals market without the influence of government control. Look at Botswana!! That country is ten years ahead of us already in terms of development because one of its key economic contributors is the diamond and the government’s limited influence and monopoly in the natural resources’ fraternity. The current Minerals Council also has to be dismantled and a new minerals board appointed. We cannot carry on having individuals who do not represent our interests as the masses having a sit in the council. All these things will awaken the economy to greater heights.
