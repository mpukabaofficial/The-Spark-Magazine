The 2023 Preliminary elections: An exodus of “unfulfilled” promises!

(From the Spark editorial desk: Mlandvo Sikhumbuzo kaMavimbela)

Last edited: August 31, 2023

Thabisile Ndlela, a 55-year-old widow residing under the Kwaluseni constituency has been living in a wretched manner of state since the demise of her husband ten good years ago. Her day’s routine begins early in the uneasy epochs of the morning where the only option bequeathed to her is gathering her stock up and walking a good couple of kilometres to her fruits and vegetables mini stall, a dreary routine she’s been accustomed to for the past 8 years.

She barely can fulfil any of her ends meet. In a crippled economy with the recent economic bulletin published by the Ministry of Economic Planning indicating a devastating elevated inflation of 5.7% in the second quarter of 2023 alone, Ndlela is a personified statistic amongst the 70% of individuals living subordinate the poverty margin. Residing in a poorly structured rented house in the Mbhuleni fragment of the Kwaluseni Inkhundla , electricity is a foreign commodity she has been struggling to afford for 3 weeks! Adding to the already thorn-pierced scar of the ordinary electorate, the Eswatini Electricity Company added a whooping 6.6% tariff on the price of electricity units in April this year. Thabisile is one of the many politically parochial electorates whose votes have been exploited by legislators without fulfilling any of their manifesto promises by the time they leave legislative office.

Enthusiastic about voting, Thabisile joined a multitude of people at the Swazi National school voting station, gathered to exercise their constitutional right to vote under the Tinkhundla dispensation. On the ground many people like Ndlela were fervent about casting their vote at the polls. The universal consensus among the electorate is the change they are optimistic in seeing. Fully aware that the past five years have been another medley of poor service delivery, the electorate under the Kwaluseni constituency seem sure that by virtue of crossing that ballot paper X, an array of issues will be addressed by the new set of legislators they will choose.

The poll stations were abuzz, a sight of patriotism. With the Friday raging winds threatening to put the electoral process into disrepute, the electorate raised up to the occasion and were in collective accord. The Elections and Boundaries Commission was the anchor that kept the entire process afloat. The EBC had previously pledged to provide special voting booths to disabled individuals, a promise that failed to see the light of day as no special provision was made for the differently abled electorate. Melusi Mthethwa, member of the Eswatini Disability Forum expressed his utter disappointment on the EBC’s failure to cater for the disabled electorate. “As members of the disabled community, we felt excluded from these elections and most of us could not exercise our rights enshrined in the constitution because nothing much was done to aid us to vote”, avowed Mthethwa on the Forum’s disappointment on the EBC’s failure to cater for the needs of disabled people in the Primary elections. The Coordinating Assembly of Non-profit Organisations(CANGO) which was the given the role of monitoring the voting process expressed disappointment on the manner in which the EBC failed to be inclusive of disabled people. “There is need to address the needs of people living with disabilities especially access to polling stations. Polling Stations should be made accessible to disabled people especially those physical disabled and provisions of transport be made for all disabled people to access polling stations’, read the primary elections report published on the afternoon of Monday the 28th of August.

The CANGO Preliminary report also alludes that the political system that the country operates under is a call for concern as it does not represent the democratic project in-par with international standards. “It is a recommendation of this report that Eswatini should consider constitutional and legislative changes that would allow individual merit to run alongside political party participation in the elections”. The empty promises made by the monarchical institution tying itself to a national dialogue seem to be taking the winds off the sails of the mass democratic movement in the country.

The last five legislative years were nothing but unprecedented. From a civil unrest, a worst inflation in ten years to a debauched health crisis responsible for the loss of life of many EmaSwati and the impartiality of the judicial system put under atomic scrutiny after the ruling of the incarcerated former legislators. The outgoing Mps will probably be mentioned in history as the instigators of the most thread-bared legislative eras the country has seen in years. An exodus of unfulfilled promises will be pontificated if the electorate continues to make uninformed choices when electing an MP.
