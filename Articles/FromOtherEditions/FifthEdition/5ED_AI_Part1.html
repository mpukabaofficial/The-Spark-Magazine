The Age Of AI: Going Beneath The Skin Of The Phenomenon Of AI

By Mphumelelo Khaba

As someone who has lived both in and out of eSwatini, I've noticed a peculiar mindset: we often believe we know enough about the world. This complacency in knowledge is risky, especially in fields like Artificial Intelligence (AI), where new developments are constantly overturning old understandings. We have geniuses like Albert Einstein saying the following, “The more I learn, the more I realize how much I don't know.” It makes me wonder why we are so comfortable in our knowledge and why we aren’t as curious and sceptical of our knowledge. I am not here to talk about that, but I believe it would be an interesting study. In our partial ignorance, and me being a tech enthusiast, I believe I have to share some of the developments in technology such as AI. Which in long form is called Artificial Intelligence. I would like to share some of the basics of it and share some of my thoughts in the subject and how it can improve us, as a country and also threaten our ability to improve too.

Artificial Intelligence can be described as the ability for a computer or computer-controlled robot to perform tasks associated with an intelligent being, in this case humans. AI has been in development for years and now has made major breakthroughs that is why it is a trending topic. The earliest known attempt to develop these technologies is in the 1950s to 1970s in the form of Neural Networks. Neural Networks are a set of instructions that take in a input, analyse and give out an output. But this technology, as a standalone, had some difficulties performing advanced tasks because it demanded a lot of functions to be written in order to advance it. This paved the way for Machine Learning. The idea was to teach a machine how to learn, feed it with large amounts of data, allow the machine to analyse it, learn the data and derive complex models that we also do not know. Once enough data has been fed we test if the machine is able to produce accurate results during testing. Machine learning happened between 1980s and 2010s. Deep learning emerged in 2011 to 2023s, which essentially uses neural networks to process large amounts of data. In the present day, we are using Generative AI which does the opposite of Machine Learning after being trained; Generative AI goes beyond predicting data after training but it produces new data as its output, for example, images. Note that these technologies are used simultaneously to produce the large and complex systems that exist today.

Why though? Why is it important? It does the repetitive computerized tasks reliably without fatigue, that frees up time for people to focus on high level tasks and thinking. It has also improved a lot of existing technologies such as spelling checks. Due to it being trained with large amounts of data, it is able to find patterns in complex structures. AI is one of the few technologies where you can have very large amounts of data in one place and it is able to analyse more and deeper data. It is able to learn better and improve, meaning it is becoming more and more accurate. In a nutshell it is taking human abilities to the next level.

The future of AI is as exciting as it is uncertain. It could lead to groundbreaking medical discoveries or revolutionize how we interact with technology. However, it could also bring challenges we haven't yet anticipated. As AI becomes increasingly sophisticated, the conversation around its development and use must also evolve.

In eSwatini and around the world, AI is not just a technological marvel but a catalyst for societal change. Understanding AI is the first step towards harnessing its potential for good while mitigating its risks. As we navigate this new age of AI, let's approach it with curiosity, caution, and a willingness to learn and adapt.
