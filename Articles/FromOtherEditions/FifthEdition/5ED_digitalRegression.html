Eswatini: A Land Fated By Digital Regression!

From the Spark Editorial Desk: Mlandvo- Sikhumbuzo Mavimbela)

Last edited: September 18, 2023

The global digital economy is forever evolving with artificial intelligence constantly becoming an undeniable prime constitute of daily life for global civilisation. Forty years since the government established the Swaziland Post and Telecommunications Parastatal through the SPTC statute of 1983, digital development in the country seems to be rather regressive! The digital divide seems to be widening in an era where technology has rapidly taken over the human functioning of life. In a land where goats outpopulate people, the country is portentously lagging behind with one of the weakest digital economies in Sub Saharan Africa.

The year 1994 was the most groundbreaking era in the history of technology in the Southern Hemisphere of the continent. MTN International set its corpus in the shores of South Africa introducing the country to its very first cellular mobile phone, a turning point in the digital realm of the country. A few years later in 1997 on the afore of Independence Day, King Mswati made the very first phone call as MTN South Africa had outfoxed Vodacom (previously Vodafone) in obtaining the official cellular mobile network connectivity license in the country. The interesting fact is that the SPTC had failed to convince the government that it had the capacity to establish a mobile network juxtaposition to its successful cable phone service from 1983 , a service which it had absolute monopoly over. Over the years this has propelled MTN to further a monopolistic dogma that has been a main driver of the digital inequality of the population as data has been an expensive artifact which many people can never bear to afford.

The SPTC’s attempt to dethrone MTN off its mobile network provision stature in 2010 seemed to be a new dawn that would enhance digital transformation in the country and offer consumers of cheaper technology and mobile network rates. This was through SPTC”s creation of the ‘ONE’ sim card network that would be the dawning of an new era in the terrain of mobile connectivity in the country. ONE, would also be a fixed wireless system that would contend with MTN. All seems amazing right?……in every shade, revolutionary!! right? This new era was short-lived as SPTC had breached a crucial clause in its agreement with MTN in the late nineties. This agreement known as the SPTC/MTN JVA ensured that King Mswati 111 had a substantial share in MTN. For him and the SPTC to continue enjoying the shares luxury at MTN, SPTC was disallowed by a certain clause in the agreement to never in its operations establish a mobile network connectivity service that would compete with them. This was a strategic move from MTN to pontificate an absolute totality of monopoly. The glory of the SPTC’s ONE network was obliterated by a ruling from the International Court of Arbitration in 2012 which forced SPTC to close down the ONE network operations. The lack of proper competition in the mobile network sector of the country has been the arch contributor to the retrogressing of the digital economy of the country.

The digital lagging-behind of the country is one of the chief reasons why 70% of the population will continue to languish in a wretched state of poverty. The digital revolution in the country is probably one of the slowest in the SADC region. This deprives the country of a wholesome economic growth. The Ministry of ICT has to adopt an array of policies which will oil the wheels of the digital revolution in the country. The unfortunate incompetencies of the Ministry of ICT is one of the reasons why the country could not achieve its “VISION 2022” policy of development.

If the government worked tirelessly to accelerate the pace of the diffusion of technology an array of economic productivity gains could have been long achieved in line with “VISION 2022”. The government has to break the moulds to achieve a stable digital economy. This will inject a boundless economic growth and the mass creation of employment opportunities within the technology sphere which the country dreadfully needs. Hopefully the state will consider hiring a vibrant devote ICT aficionado with an incomparable wealth of knowledge in as far as technology is concerned as an ICT Minister in the upcoming parliament. Ill-fatedly, the last five cabinet years for the ICT portfolio has been led by leader who hasn’t come to the fore in driving the mass revolution in the Digital arena of the country.
