Vision 2022: The Story Of a Country Killing its Dreams.|Part 1

Last edited: July 8, 2022

I was leaving for 2 years and I thought all things were going to be on track and nothing would deter eSwatini from its course. I couldn’t postulate a tragic event that could make a sweeping difference in the land of the Swazi people, eSwatini. After running many scenarios in my head, I was not able to foresee a disease, in this case COVID-19, that would make a grand entrance and take the reins from humanity. However, for me that wasn’t the worst part in those two years. After those two years I had to return. I landed at the airport and I was very excited by the thought that I was going to drive and witness the rapidly emerging urban areas.

The airport was so secluded it makes one think there couldn’t be a developed area in that particular country. But it was not just a particular country it was my home for nineteen years – I knew it like the back of my hand. Not because I am equating the size of my hand to the size of my country or vice versa, although it is a relatively small country. I knew that as I would have been driving, eventually, I would enter civilization; and, at that moment, I was hypnotized anticipating how much it had developed.

"The slogans always gave me hope: “Vision 2022!” "

My disappointment stems from the events that preceded me leaving eSwatini. There were ever so repeating slogans on the radio in the mornings as I rode the bus to school. EmaSwati know this as Quantum or Kombi. If it weren’t for my everyday morning commutes, I never would have listened to the radio otherwise. I am glad I did because I got to be inspired and my favourite part were the slogans. The slogans always gave me hope: “Vision 2022!” I held on to that promise and told myself that one day I will be riding a Quantum and there will be tall buildings all over the place and the Swazi people would be flourishing left and right. The harvest of our work – or maybe the king’s , as others speculated – would have been bountiful. I thought maybe, just maybe, the dream would be attained. I wanted that. I was rooting for that. That thought parallels the ride from the airport back home. On the way home my cheeks could not stop visiting my ears as I saw the roads being built and massive improvement was occurring.

However, I was not fully satisfied with how fast the progress was being made. In all the excitement and the motivation, a part of me was being contaminated with the pessimistic thought that we might be too slow. Actually, very, very slow. To explain how impactful this tiny negative feeling was, imagine my happiness being drinking water in a 20-litre bucket. The cynicism is like a drop of poison added to that water – probably it would not injure a person who drinks it but the stakes are just too high. Neither could I stay excited! Probably the wave of advancement was on its way and so I asked questions. I was constantly asking the wrong ones until it came to me: “Why wouldn’t I just ask about the 2022 vision?”

"I was constantly asking the wrong ones until it came to me: “Why wouldn’t I just ask about the 2022 vision?” "

I wish I never asked that. It was a big mistake. “Wait what? 2022 is gone?” that was me thinking it was a joke, a bad one I might add. If it were for me, such a joke should be illegalized, people should not be allowed to make a joke about the vision 2022. Especially if they say that it is dead. It was sacred to my heart. To my surprise, it wasn’t a joke. I felt like the country waited for me to leave and it just tossed the vision into the abyss and moved on. Why me though? Eswatini had killed its dream to become a developed country by year 2022. I was only gone for two years and returned in 2021. Those were the worst news I had received by that time.

What bugged me is not whether we could or we could not reach it. Even if after the deadline, we would have made it one percent of the way. I would be okay about it. What I could not wrap my mind around was the idea that an entire country could conveniently let go of a dream without fighting. They raised the white flag before they failed and mindlessly felt good about it. They thought by quitting earlier they would avoid failing and I hope you also realize the irony in that. Quitting guaranteed the failure inevitable but we should not have done that. We should have tried and failed. We should have been willing to endure the embarrassment of failing hard.

"We should have tried and failed. We should have been willing to endure the embarrassment of failing hard"

There is something sinister about a whole society willing to put away a dream and its ambitions. It clearly shows the extent at which each individual, liSwati, is aspirational. For me it screamed that the statistics were: “… over 50% of emaSwati are willing to quit…” I hope I am completely wrong. I hope I am way off track, I hope that my logic is absurd and the fact is that I am crazy to even come up with this conclusion. If I am right, disaster! This kind of mindset brews terror in my mind because I hope emaSwati would have some love for the country. Just a little bit. I wish the people of eSwatini could have a Spark of love for eSwatini in their heart. I think ownership and the feeling of responsibility of making the eSwatini, my home, better than yesterday has a promise of great rewards.
